why this guide exists

Finding housing in Korea is exciting — but also confusing. Through interviews with international students at Korea University, three pain points kept coming up.

📋

Unfamiliar Contract Systems

Korea has rental structures unlike anything in most countries. Jeonse, Wolse, Ban-jeonse — most students had never heard these terms before arriving.

🗣️

Language Barriers

Contracts, maintenance requests, residence reports — all in Korean. One wrong misunderstanding can cost you your deposit.

🏠

Cultural Surprises

Semi-basement apartments, strict noise etiquette, complex garbage sorting — Korean apartment life has its own rules, and no one explains them upfront.

Rental Systems

chapter 1 · how renting works in korea

Korea has three rental structures. The key insight: a deposit is not a fee — it is money temporarily held and returned to you at the end of the lease.

전세

Jeonse

DepositVery Large
often ₩100M+
Monthly RentNone
Best ForLong-term residents with large savings
RiskHigh — deposit return risk
Most Common for Students
월세

Wolse

DepositSmaller
~₩5M–₩30M
Monthly RentYes — paid monthly
Best ForStudents and short-term residents
RiskLower upfront risk
반전세

Ban-jeonse

DepositMedium-large
~₩30M–₩100M
Monthly RentYes — but smaller
Best ForResidents with moderate savings
RiskMedium

You hand the landlord a very large sum — often the price of a small apartment — and live there for one or two years paying nothing per month. At the end, the landlord returns that deposit in full.

Why does the landlord agree? While you're living there, they have free use of your money — to invest, repay loans, or do whatever. So Jeonse isn't free housing; you're lending the landlord a small fortune in exchange for somewhere to live.

The risk: if the landlord can't pay you back at the end, you're in serious trouble. Before signing any Jeonse contract, check the property register, verify existing mortgages, and confirm the landlord's identity.

Deposit Protection

chapter 2 · how to protect your money

Your deposit can be protected by law — but only if you follow these four steps after moving in.

1

Move In

Actually take physical possession of the property.

Banjiha — Semi-Basement Apartments

chapter 3 · a uniquely korean housing type

반지하 (Banjiha) apartments sit partly below ground level. They're found all over Seoul — even in desirable neighborhoods — and can be a cost-effective option if you know what to check.

Advantages

  • Lower rent than equivalent above-ground units
  • Often in well-located, central neighborhoods
  • Cooler in summer (partially insulated by earth)

Disadvantages

  • High humidity — mold is a common issue
  • Poor natural light and ventilation
  • Windows at or below street level — limited privacy
  • Flooding risk during heavy rainfall
Photos lie. Listings lie.
A walk-through doesn't. Always visit in person before signing — check the walls for mold stains, run the bathroom fan, smell for damp. Visit after rain if you can.

Change of Residence Report

chapter 5 · a legal obligation, not a suggestion

When you move, you have 15 days to report your new address. Miss the deadline and you can be fined.
💻

Online — HiKorea

Available 24/7. Go to hikorea.go.kr → Civil Service → Change of Address Report.

Fastest option. No travel required.

🏢

In Person — Immigration Office

Visit your nearest Immigration Office during business hours.

Bring: Passport, Alien Registration Card, and Form 34 (Change-of-Residence form).

No service charge

Both methods are free. Just do it within the first week of moving — don't let it slip past the 15-day window.

Apartment Etiquette

chapter 6 · being a good neighbor

Korean apartment buildings are stacked tight. Walls are thinner than you think, and you'll see the same faces in the elevator for years. Good apartment living is mostly mutual respect.

🔇

Noise

  • Lower volume after 10 PM
  • Run washing machine and vacuum during the day — not at night
  • Stick felt pads on chair and table legs
  • Wear slippers indoors — heel strikes on concrete travel straight down
🗑️

Garbage Sorting

Korea has six categories with separate bags and fees. Food waste is the tricky one:

ItemFood Waste?
Banana peel✓ Yes
Corn husk / silk✗ No
Apple core✓ Yes
Peach / avocado pit✗ No
Eggshell✗ No
Tea bag✗ No
Onion skin✗ No

When in doubt, ask the building manager.

🚗

Parking

  • Spots are often unassigned — double-parking is normal
  • If you double-park: leave gear in neutral, parking brake off — so neighbors can push your car
  • Keep your phone reachable when double-parked
🤝

General

  • Keep shared spaces clean — corridors, lobby, laundry room
  • Clean up after your pet
  • Smoke only in permitted areas — never on the veranda
  • Greet your neighbors
안녕하세요 Annyeonghaseyo That one word does more for apartment life than anything else on this list.
